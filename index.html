DISPELLING MYTHS ABOUT

REMITTANCES

A STORY, AND A MYTH

As of 2017, 8, 10, and 30 percent of Guatemalan, Honduran, and El Salvadorian working-age populations, respectively, resided outside their home countries, with most residing in the United States.

As a result, in 2018, remittance flows to the region from emigrants exceeded 19 billion USD, accounting for 12.1, 20.1, and 20.7 percent, respectively, of GDP in Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador. [1]

At this point, a leap in rhetoric is often made: that Northern Triangle immigrants, who often migrate irregularly, are draining resources and capital from the United States to fuel extravagance in their home country, to build an upper-class wealth. When such misinterpretation transforms to disinformation, the result fuels anti-immigrant sentiment, unpredictable swings in immigration policy, and little improvement in the available regular pathways for migration.

But this conclusion is far from the truth: to illustrate why, we have to characterize the households in the Northern Triangle that receive remittances, and what precisely the remittances pay for.

So we found ourselves asking, what is the average monthly remittance amount received by households in the Northern Triangle by country and urban-rural classification, and for what purposes is this money spent?





THE STUDY

Luckily, a recent survey conducted by the World Food Programme helps unearth this information.

Of the nearly 5000 participants of the survey, which asks questions ranging from relationships to those who emigrated, to plans of the survey participant of ever migrating, to food security and well-being, 1447 respondents reported that they received remittances from someone abroad. Of these, 709 respondents received remittances on a regular basis, and graciously consented to providing detailed information about their household’s use of this money. We can try to understand from their responses how remittances are generally used in the Northern Triangle.

Here, we represent each of these 709 households, each of these families in the Northern Triangle Region, as a circle. You can hover over each to learn about the amount their household receives in remittances on average per month, as well as their self-reported household income. Let’s try to discern some information from this survey.




A VIEW OF REMITTANCE-RECEIVING HOUSEHOLDS BY COUNTRY

Of the 709 respondents,
  • 220 said they lived in Guatemala. They on average received 125.33 USD/mo in remittances.
  • 151 said they lived in Honduras. They on averaged received 144.21 USD/mo.
  • 338 responded saying they lived in El Salvador, receiving on average 135.71 USD/mo.


For comparison, for a family of four, the average monthly cost of living excluding rent is 2,097.67 USD in Guatemala, 1,884.52 USD in Honduras, and 2,029.16 USD in El Salvador. [3]

A VIEW OF REMITTANCE-RECEIVING HOUSEHOLDS IN RURAL vs. URBAN AREAS

Of the 709 respondents, 450 were from rural areas, and 259 from urban.

Rural-area participants received a similar amount in remittances per month on average, according to self-report, to urban-area participants, about 134.79 USD/mo compared to 133.44 USD/mo.











Now let’s view what each household reported they use remittance money towards. When asked about the expenses remittances cover, survey participants had the following options with which to respond, in decreasing order of response frequency:

  1. food,
  2. utilities,
  3. health,
  4. clothes and shoes,
  5. education,
  6. housing,
  7. savings,
  8. migration loan debt,
  9. agriculture,
  10. housing,
  11. other debts,
  12. business ventures,
  13. home purchase, and
  14. commitments made on behalf of migrants.
  15. other,




To answer the question of how remittances are most often used, let's visualize the number of survey respondents using remittance funds towards the most basic needs like food, utilities like light, water, and mobile communication, and health, and then look at needs just beyond those, like clothing and education. We noticed that less than 5 percent of respondents reported using remittance money towards each of the other eleven categories.









HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

FOOD

A majority of respondents, 574 out of 709 (80.96%), reported spending remittance money on food.

HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

UTILITIES

296 respondents (41.75%) said some remittance money goes towards utility expenses.

HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

HEALTH

252 respondents (35.54%) said some of the money goes towards health expenses.

HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

EDUCATION

In comparison, a mere 51 (7.19%) are able to report putting remittance money towards education expenses in the household.

HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

CLOTHING

Similarly, only 61 of the 709 (8.60%) participants report putting remittance money to clothing and shoes.





HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

Housing

Finally, only 35 of the 709 (4.94%) participants report spending remittance money towards housing.





HOW MANY HOUSEHOLDS SPEND REMITTANCE MONEY ON...

Other Expenses

Finally, only 43 of the 709 (6.06%) households report spending remittance money towards other expenses such as agricultural inputs, business ventures, savings, the Migrant's financial commitments', debts and other undefined expenses.

















WHAT HAVE WE LEARNT?
We have learnt that the story of remittances runs deeper than the myth: as recently as 2021, Northern Triangle households report using this money towards covering the most basic of needs.

So what can we do about it?
  • We need to be aware of this when supporting radical shifts in migration policy that disrupt this critical income source.
  • We need compassion in our discussions of the migration and development in the Northern Triangle, understanding how those on the ground are directly impacted.
  • We need to acknowledge the impact of remittances in our discussions of improving pathways to regular migration to the United States for those who would like to.
